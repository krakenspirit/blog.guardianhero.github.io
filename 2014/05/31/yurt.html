Yurts

May 31, 2014

While I was volunteering at the horse rescue, I met a woman and shared with her my original plans involving a gypsy wagon and horses. I told her of two people I knew who did traveled unconventionally. Bob Skelding and a John Sears who traveled by donkey. I got a chance to encounter the latter when he passed through my state. The woman was fascinated by my plans and asked me if I had ever heard of a ‘yurt’. I hadn’t. I went home and looked it up and thought, wow. What if I could build a yurt and travel with my car and live with that!

This is a picture of what I imagined (and still imagine) my lifestyle for my next few years to be like. Having a portable lifestyle, where I live free, volunteer and work exchanging while having more time to write, web develop and be there for my family. Building my monastery and training to be a Guardian Monk. The only thing I didn’t anticipate was how difficult the yurt would be transport without a family.

I contacted Hal who ran Laurel Nest Yurts at the time with his wife Asia (pronounced Osha). We struck a deal and he helped me build my own yurt. Unfortunately yurts are hard to transport with a little vehicle, and even harder to put up by yourself (impossible). While I LOVE the yurt, and do plan to use it when my children are old enough to help me take it down. I had to upgrade my plan, and about a year later I discovered the Bell Tent. The closest thing to a portable yurt I think I will ever get. At present I still work on the yurt I built, and when I purchase my land will get a chance to live on it while building yurt styled earth-bag houses.
